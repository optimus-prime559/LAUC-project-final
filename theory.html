Theory

Heat conduction is the transfer of thermal energy through a material without any motion of the material as a whole. It occurs due to temperature gradients within solids, liquids, or gases. Fourier’s Law provides the fundamental relationship governing heat conduction in different geometries.

Fourier’s Law for Plane Walls

Q = −kA (dT / dx)

The rate of heat transfer through a plane wall is proportional to the negative temperature gradient and the area normal to heat flow.

Heat Conduction Through Cylindrical Pipes

For a long cylindrical pipe with inner radius r₁, outer radius r₂, length L, and constant thermal conductivity k, the heat transfer rate under steady-state conditions is given by:

Q = [2πkL (T₁ − T₂)] / ln(r₂ / r₁)

T₁ is the temperature at inner radius r₁ and T₂ is the temperature at outer radius r₂

In cylindrical coordinates, heat flows radially from the inner surface to the outer surface (or vice versa). The temperature distribution in cylindrical systems is logarithmic due to the variation of heat transfer area with radius.

Q = −kAr (dT / dr)

For cylindrical coordinates, Ar = 2πrL

Heat Conduction in Plane Walls

In plane walls, heat flows in a straight line perpendicular to the surface. The temperature distribution is linear under steady-state conditions with constant thermal conductivity. This geometry is commonly encountered in building walls, flat plates, and slabs.

Heat Conduction in Cylindrical Pipes

In cylindrical pipes, heat flows radially from the inner surface to the outer surface. The temperature distribution is logarithmic and the heat transfer area increases with radius. This is important in pipelines, heat exchangers, and insulated cables.

Comparison of Heat Conduction Geometries

Aspect Plane Wall Cylindrical Pipe
Coordinate System Cartesian (x, y, z) Cylindrical (r, θ, z)
Heat Flow Direction Linear (one-dimensional) Radial (one-dimensional)
Temperature Distribution Linear Logarithmic
Heat Transfer Area Constant Varies with radius (2πrL)
Heat Transfer Rate Q = kA(T₁ − T₂)/L Q = 2πkL(T₁ − T₂)/ln(r₂/r₁)
Typical Applications Walls, flat plates, slabs Pipelines, heat exchangers, insulated cables

Sample Problem: Insulated Pipe

A steel pipe (k = 45 W/m·K) with inner radius 50 mm and outer radius 60 mm carries hot fluid at 180°C. The pipe is covered with 40 mm thick insulation (k = 0.1 W/m·K). If the outer surface temperature is 30°C, calculate the heat loss per meter length.

Result: The calculated heat loss is approximately 203.9 W/m, demonstrating that insulation significantly reduces heat loss compared to an uninsulated pipe.
